Exercise 7.

In Equation (8.3.11), we learned that for an infinite geometric series
\begin{equation*} S = a + ar + ar^n + \cdots + ar^{n-1} + \cdots\text{,} \end{equation*}
if \(|r| \lt 1\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} S = \frac{a}{1-r}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This result provides a quick way to determine the value of an infinite geometric series that converges.
🔗
In this exercise, we explore why the infinite geometric series fails to converge when \(r = 1\text{,}\) \(r = -1\text{,}\) and \(|r|>1\text{.}\)
🔗
  1. If we consider the finite geometric series that results when \(a = 1\) and \(r = 1\text{,}\) we get the sum
    \begin{equation*} S_n = 1 + 1 + \cdots + 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where there are \(n\) terms in the sum.

  2. Next, consider the finite geometric series that results when \(a = 1\) and \(r = -1\text{,}\) which is the sum
    \begin{equation*} S_n = 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + \cdots + (-1)^{n-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where there are \(n\) terms in the sum. (We view the first “\(1\)” as resulting from “\(1 \cdot (-1)^0\)”.)
